1. Rename Strategy & Brand Alignment
The rename to "GutChecks: Red Flags & Safety" solves the app store confusion immediately. At least three
other apps use "GutCheck" — a food/gut-health tracker on both iOS and Google Play, a colonoscopy booking app
(GutChek), and a market research platform (GutCheck/Toluna). The subtitle "Red Flags & Safety" signals purpose
before download and improves ASO.
Rename Checklist
All touchpoints: App Store listing, website header/footer, meta titles, Open Graph tags, social profiles, email
signatures, in-app references
Domain: Keep mygutcheck.org but secure gutchecksapp.com or gutchecks.app as a redirect if available
App Store keywords: "red flags", "safety", "manipulation", "toxic", "gaslighting", "grooming", "radicalisation",
"scam"
Capitalisation: Always "GutChecks" (capital G, capital C) — never "Gutchecks" or "gutchecks"
Logo: Update to "GutChecks" with "Red Flags & Safety" as a visible tagline beneath
Social handles: Secure @gutchecksapp on TikTok, Instagram, X, Snapchat, YouTube immediately
User count: Use "1,000+ users" consistently across all platforms until a higher verified milestone is reached

10. Founder Story — Review & Placement
Overall assessment: This is a genuinely compelling founder story. It is personal, specific, emotionally honest, and
directly connected to the product mission. It has the ingredients to be a strong asset for press, fundraising,
partnership outreach, and the About page.
What works well
• The personal trigger — discovering your daughter's search history — is vivid and immediately relatable to
any parent.
• The professional credibility (Royal Navy veteran, Goldman Sachs) is genuinely impressive and differentiates
you from typical app founders.
• The "there was no vaccine" framing is strong — it positions GutChecks as prevention rather than reaction.
• The decision to quit Goldman Sachs to build this signals serious commitment.
• The community angle ("I can protect my child... but if the people she interacts with are not protected")
broadens the mission beyond personal motivation.
What needs tightening
"masterclass in controlled chaos"
This opening line is dramatic but vague. Start with something more grounded: "For years, I balanced two
demanding worlds — raising my daughter as a single mother while serving in the Royal Navy and later working in
compliance at Goldman Sachs."
"I was building a fortress she couldn't get into"
Powerful metaphor but may feel overly self-critical in a public context. Consider softening to: "I was so focused on
providing for her that I did not see what she was going through."
"Her answer broke me in a way no military training or corporate pressure ever could"
Effective emotionally but reads slightly over-written for a professional bio. Consider: "Her answer stopped me cold."
Statistics paragraph
The stats (89% increase, 20 crimes per day) are strong but feel dropped in. Integrate them more smoothly or move
them to a separate "The Problem" section. In the founder story, the personal narrative should carry the weight.
"I quit my job at Goldman Sachs"
This is your strongest credibility signal. Make sure it lands clearly — perhaps as its own short paragraph.
"It's not a monitoring app. It's an educational tool..."
Good distinction. Keep this — it directly addresses the parental surveillance concern.
Spelling: "recognise" vs "recognize"
The story mixes British and American spelling. Standardise to British English throughout (recognise, behaviour,
defence) since the company is UK-based.
Where to place the founder story
Primary placement: /about page. This should be the centrepiece of the About page, alongside a professional
photo and the advisory board section (see Section 11). A condensed version (3–4 sentences) should also appear
on the homepage — ideally between the testimonials and the final CTA.
Secondary uses: Press kit (full version), App Store "About the Developer" section (condensed), pitch deck
(condensed), social media (pull-quote excerpts).
Suitability for awards/funding applications: Yes — with the tightening above. The story is specific, personal,
and mission-driven. For awards or grant applications, emphasise: (1) the personal trigger, (2) the gap in the market
(prevention vs reaction), (3) your unique professional background (military discipline + financial risk management +
product skills), and (4) the community mission. Remove or reduce the emotional flourishes and let the facts speak.
Recommended condensed version (for homepage)
"GutChecks was born from a real moment. As a single mother, Royal Navy veteran, and former Goldman Sachs
manager, I thought I had every risk covered — until I discovered my 11-year-old daughter had been silently
navigating confusing and pressuring interactions online, turning to equally confused friends rather than coming to
me. I searched for a tool that could help her recognise red flags before they escalated. It did not exist. So I built it.
GutChecks is the prevention tool I wish I had — designed to help young people trust their instincts and spot
manipulation before it becomes harm."

13. Conversion Recommendations
Structural and UX changes beyond copy that will improve conversion.
1. Add a product demo or walkthrough video
Young users want to see the product before committing. A 30–60 second screen recording showing a real
interaction being analysed would dramatically increase conversion. Place in "How It Works". This is the single
highest-impact conversion improvement.
2. Add real screenshots or mockups
The site has zero product visuals. No screenshots, no mockups, no UI previews. Visitors have no idea what they
are downloading. Add 3–4 screenshots: (1) conversation input, (2) red flag analysis output, (3) pattern tracking
view, (4) helpline/resources screen.
3. Add a "Try before you sign up" flow
Offer one free analysis without account creation. Let cautious young users experience value before committing.
Even a limited demo reduces bounce rate.
4. Move privacy higher on the page
For a product targeting young people with sensitive topics, privacy should be the first thing after the headline. Move
the trust strip directly beneath the hero.
5. Fix the Android gap
The footer says "Available on Android and iOS" but Google Play says "Coming Soon". Either remove the Android
claim or publish the app. Inconsistency erodes trust.
6. Add social proof matching the audience
For young users, TikTok-style short video testimonials are far more persuasive than text quotes. Consider
embedding 2–3 TikTok reviews or creating a "Stories" section with anonymised case examples.
7. Reduce CTA friction
Every CTA links to the App Store. Add a web-based entry point — a simple form where someone can paste a
message and get a preview analysis. Not everyone wants to download before understanding.
8. Add age-appropriate onboarding cues
For 13–17, add: "You can use GutChecks on your own — or show it to a parent or trusted adult." Signals safety for
both user and any adult who might see the site.
9. Copyright year
Footer says "© 2025 GutCheck" — update to "© 2025–2026 GutChecks".
10. Founder story on homepage
Add a condensed 3–4 sentence founder bio between testimonials and the final CTA. A real human behind the
product dramatically increases trust. See Section 10.
